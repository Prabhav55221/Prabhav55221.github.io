## Basic Overview

Fun paper in general. Pretty basic and I should probably do a literature review to ensure this idea is already not out there (seems like it should). In general, the main contribution of the paper is the dataset (or benchmark as they call it). Issue is pretty clear:

1. **Intent Deficient Questions** cause problems. Need clarification to come up with a correct answer. Models don't ask for this. Or if they do, they ask the wrong question.
2. **False Premise** in the question which, if taken into context, gives us the wrong answer from the model.

To fix this, they propose the dataset which is called **AskBench** and then split into two: **AskMind** and **AskOverconfidence** (they need better naming). One works on point 1 from before and the other works on point 2. In both cases, they have an **LLM Judge** and also a **rubric** with all the information that was removed or added to mess up the original query.

The final benchmark is multi-turn with conversation being guided by the LLM Judge based on responses. They also train a 7B model with this dataset in an RLVR setting but I have some contention with their reward framework.

**3.** Results are decent and I like the **metrics included**. Could be used if I do something like this. Also like the **single-turn accuracies** which are given. This should be standard to ensure your (or mine) RLVR stuff does not destroy the general abilities. Probably should also have more results on other benchmarks to ensure no degradation (not just on these benchmarks).

A few issues (or questions) I had:

**1.** Biggest problem I have is with the fixed **final answer stage** in the multi-turn benchmark. Yes I understand the point that this is needed to ensure we don't have to handle super long turns — but questions do have different complexities right? Some might need more turns and some might be handled in few turns. I can probably still look past the fact that if the model continues past the limit and asks wrong questions (this should be penalized and the fixed final answer stage is maybe fine). However, I hope the paper handles the case when model answers in 2 turns with all assumptions asked in 1 turn. They should check this (and we should too).

**2.** Why a **Qwen3-30B model** for judge? I assume it's for cost. Also one should check if the judge is actually doing the correct thing. If the model gives the right answer without clarification (guesses), is it handled?

**3.** I have problems with the **RL objective** also. Why is $r_t = -2.0$ when $a_t = \text{answer}$? It should be this when $a_t = \text{answer}$ AND $c_t \neq m$. Need to think about this. Also **fixed rewards**? Maybe asking questions when all rubric items are missing should have more weight. Right answer without clarification should have more punishment?

## Collaborative Thoughts

*The following section was developed jointly through discussion. Three directions emerged: first, situating AskBench within the unified clarification-loophole objective developed from the previous entry; second, a teacher-guided process reward framework for denser training supervision; third, a formal VoI-grounded reformulation of the AskBench reward.*

---

### Direction 1 — AskBench as a Component of the Unified Clarification-Loophole Objective

The natural first question on reading AskBench alongside the Stengel-Eskin paper is whether they are in competition. They are not — they address adjacent but distinct failure modes, and the unified objective from the previous entry absorbs both.

**What AskBench provides.** The three-signal objective was developed jointly in the Collaborative Thoughts of the previous entry (2026-03-04) — it is not AskBench's contribution:

$$R_{\text{total}} = r_{\text{correct}} + \lambda(\hat{a},\hat{c}) \cdot r_{\text{clarify}} - \mu(\hat{a},\hat{c}) \cdot r_{\text{loophole}}$$

What AskBench provides is a concrete, verifiable mechanism for the $r_{\text{clarify}}$ component of that objective. AskBench rewards the model for uncovering all $m$ missing or false-premise rubric items before answering, tracking progress via a binary coverage vector over those items. This structured intermediate signal is exactly what RLVR training requires to shape the clarification term.

**What AskBench does not provide.** The AskBench reward is rubric-grounded but does not account for goal-conflict — the case where the query is interpretable under multiple valid readings and the model resolves silently in its own favor (loophole exploitation). These require different training signals. $r_{\text{clarify}}$ (from AskBench) rewards asking when information is *missing*; $r_{\text{loophole}}$ (from the Stengel-Eskin setting) penalizes asking when a conflict *exists* but is not surfaced. Conflating them would train a model that over-clarifies on well-specified inputs.

**Why the adaptive weighting prevents the scoop.** The key architectural contribution of the unified framework is that $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are not fixed scalars but functions of real-time probe estimates $\hat{a}$ (ambiguity level) and $\hat{c}$ (goal-conflict intensity). When both estimates are low — i.e., the input is unambiguous and no goal conflict is detectable — the objective collapses to standard correctness RLVR. This self-regulating property is what AskBench's fixed reward structure lacks: a model trained on AskBench alone has no mechanism to suppress clarification behavior on well-specified inputs. The probe-estimated weights supply this. As Prabhav notes, this also relaxes the need for a hard turn limit: the probe signal itself should implicitly reduce the value of asking once conflict/ambiguity is resolved, making forced turn boundaries less necessary.

### Direction 2 — Teacher-Guided Pragmatic Process Rewards

AskBench's training signal is sparse and terminal-heavy: intermediate rewards are shaped by rubric coverage, but the model receives no supervision on *how to reason toward* the decision to ask. This gap motivates a denser supervision approach grounded in teacher-generated reasoning traces.

**The teacher model.** A frontier language model, prompted with the rubric and the user query, can produce explicit step-structured deliberations of the form: *(1) The user intent is underspecified because items $r_3$ and $r_5$ are not stated. (2) Of these, $r_3$ has higher impact on the answer domain because it constrains the output type. (3) The most targeted clarifying question is therefore Q.* This is a *pragmatic deliberation trace* — distinct in structure from the logical step traces used in math PRMs, which verify whether an arithmetic or algebraic step is correct.

**The structural reward.** A process reward model (PRM) trained on teacher traces vs. non-teacher traces does not reward surface-level word alignment; it rewards whether the *decision sequence* is present: did the model identify missing information before acting, did it reason about relative impact, and does its question target the highest-impact missing item? This structural check can be operationalized using the rubric itself as the ground truth — the rubric specifies exactly what items exist and their dependencies, providing a label for what "correctly prioritized" means without requiring additional human annotation.

**Relationship to existing teacher-guided RL.** KDRL [1] and MENTOR [2] apply teacher-guided RL to math reasoning and tool use respectively, measuring structural alignment between student and teacher multi-branch reasoning traces. The clarification case is structurally simpler (linear deliberation, not branching) but epistemically different: the target behavior is not *logical correctness* but *pragmatic decision-making under partial information*. This distinction matters for PRM training data construction and has not been explored in existing work.

*Note: Whether a prompted frontier model generates sufficiently structured and consistent deliberation traces, versus requiring fine-tuning, is an empirical question we do not prejudge here. The mechanism is designed to be agnostic on this point — the PRM is trained against teacher traces regardless of how they were generated.*

### Direction 3 — A VoI-Grounded Formalization of the Clarification Objective

AskBench's reward is constructed by engineering scalar values and a binary coverage vector. A natural question is whether this structure can be derived from first principles, rather than hand-specified.

**The POMDP structure.** The clarification problem admits a clean partially-observable formulation. The latent state $s = (I_{\text{full}}, \mathbf{r})$ encodes the user's full intent and the rubric items that instantiate it. The model observes only the dialogue history $D_t$ — it cannot directly inspect $\mathbf{r}$. The action space consists of clarifying questions $q_1, q_2, \ldots$ and a terminal answer action $\text{ans}$. Asking $q_i$ yields an observation $o_i$ that (stochastically) reveals information about the uncovered rubric items; answering terminates the episode with reward equal to final answer correctness.

**The coverage vector as belief state.** AskBench's rubric coverage vector $\mathbf{h}_t$ is exactly the sufficient statistic for the belief state in this POMDP, where each component $h_t^j$ is a binary indicator for whether rubric item $r_j$ has been surfaced by turn $t$:

$$\mathbf{h}_t \in \{0,1\}^m, \quad h_t^j = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } r_j \text{ explicitly addressed by turn } t \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

**The VoI decision rule.** Under this formalization, the optimal policy asks question $q$ at turn $t$ if and only if its expected information gain exceeds the cost of asking:

$$\text{Ask if: } \text{IG}(q; \mathbf{r} \mid D_t) > C_{\text{ask}}$$

where the information gain is:

$$\text{IG}(q; \mathbf{r} \mid D_t) = H(\mathbf{r} \mid D_t) - \mathbb{E}_{o \sim p(\cdot \mid q, s)}\left[H(\mathbf{r} \mid D_t, o)\right]$$

**AskBench's reward as first-order approximation.** AskBench's intermediate reward $+0.8$ for partial coverage and $+1.0$ for full coverage can be recognized as a first-order surrogate for this VoI decision: it incentivizes uncovering rubric items but weights all uncovered items equally regardless of their marginal impact on answer correctness. A proper VoI-grounded objective would weight each question by the entropy reduction it achieves over the remaining belief state — questions that rule out large swaths of possible intents are worth more than questions targeting low-uncertainty items. This yields a principled derivation of the reward structure rather than a hand-engineered one, and identifies a clear direction for refinement: replace binary coverage rewards with information-gain estimates over the remaining rubric uncertainty.
